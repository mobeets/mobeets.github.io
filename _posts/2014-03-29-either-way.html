When the air is thicker they can fly through the city silently. Amusing only themselves— forming care-free zig-zags, now spirals. One gives the signal and they fly in rectangles.
On edge, we give them glances of fear and expectation. And the birds, sensing this tension, decide they might now like sitting still more than moving. One by two they break off from the pack, landing on post boxes... the traffic poles near busy intersections... anywhere they might catch a good show.
Time passes. And still, the birds are unsatisfied. But they say nothing aloud. Their anticipation not yet worth mentioning— this they know instinctively.

Never rub my hands under seat cushions, never step in gum. Always check my shoes... to confirm that they are still tied.
But behind these minor defences, there is of course the blushing. My major flaw: that perpetual look of embarrassment... foiling any attempts at looking stolid.
One morning, I dream of two grackles perched outside my window. I hear their words but not their conversation. They seem sinister, calculating. I oversleep, and skip breakfast to catch the bus to work that morning.

Work was uneventful, but I ignored the dream until I was headed home. What was it they were saying about me? They looked unusually intelligent —like they knew something I didn't.
The bus feels suffocating, and I get off planning to catch the next one. But even at the bus stop I feel uneasy... like there's someone standing close behind me. The idea alone repulses me, and my entire body flinches. The grackles near me shriek as the blood fills my face.

Either way, I think, I'd never asked for an audience. I'd never been the type to tell a story you didn't ask to hear. Maybe it's unavoidable that I feel like this. Maybe there's nothing I can do. All the same, I think, if someone could just let me down...
